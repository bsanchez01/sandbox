Key Insights & Analysis
Data-driven observations for academic leadership decision-making
32.3%
High System-Wide Failure Rate
Nearly 1-in-3 completed student records result in a failing grade. With 2,657 failures across 8,226 completions, this represents a systemic concern requiring structured intervention at both course and program levels.
+13.6pp
Summer Performance Surge
Summer 2025 posted a 78.5% pass rate — over 13 percentage points above Fall and Spring (~64.5%). Summer cohorts tend to be self-selected and motivated, but structural factors in delivery likely also contribute.
ENG 097
Highest-Risk High-Volume Course
ENG 097 had 487 enrollments — the largest of any course — with only a 35.8% pass rate. This single course accounts for 309 failures, a disproportionate share of the institution's total fail count.
7 courses
Critical Failure Zone (<40% Pass)
Seven significant courses (10+ enrolled) fell below 40% pass rate, including SCI 281 (25%), ASL 101 (33.3%), and ENG 097 (35.8%). These represent acute risk areas requiring curriculum and pedagogical review this coming year.
10× gap
Wide Faculty Performance Spread
Faculty pass rates span 25% (Sinkey, David) to 100% (Savu, Waisake; Rainibogi, Marica; Hugo, Bugoro). This 75-point spread signals highly variable instructional effectiveness and warrants structured peer-support programs.
98.3%
Students Stay — But Don't Always Succeed
Despite high failure rates, 98.3% of enrolled students complete through the term. The failure problem is academic performance, not dropout — students are present but not passing, which is a focused and actionable challenge.

Actionable Recommendations
Five priority actions for academic leadership — ordered by estimated institutional impact
01
Curriculum & Instruction
Redesign the Developmental English Pipeline (ENG 086, 087, 096, 097)
With 1,400+ combined enrollments and pass rates averaging below 47%, the entire developmental English sequence is failing students at scale. Recommend a task force to conduct a full curriculum audit, introduce co-requisite support models, reduce class sizes, and pilot contextualized literacy instruction tied to students' fields of study. This single intervention could improve the institutional pass rate by multiple percentage points.
02
Faculty Development
Structured Peer-Mentorship for Faculty Below 50% Pass Rate
Eight faculty members post pass rates below 50%. Implement a structured coaching program pairing these instructors with high performers (above 85%). Share best practices in assessment design, early-warning identification, and student engagement. Add confidential mid-semester student feedback surveys in these sections to provide timely, actionable course-correction data.
03
Student Support
Deploy Early-Alert Intervention for the Top 15 Highest-Risk Course Sections
Assign dedicated academic support coaches to the highest-risk sections at week 3. Mandate progress check-ins at weeks 5 and 8. Students in courses with historical pass rates below 60% should be proactively flagged for tutoring, counseling, and structured study group access. Early-stage interventions consistently outperform end-of-semester rescue strategies in reducing final failure counts.
04
Policy & Scheduling
Investigate and Replicate the Summer 2025 Success Model
Summer 2025's 78.5% pass rate significantly outperformed Fall and Spring. A targeted analysis of Summer's course offerings, faculty assignments, enrollment caps, and student demographics should pinpoint the structural drivers. If smaller class sizes, specific faculty, or a focused course catalog are responsible, these elements should inform Fall and Spring scheduling decisions for AY 2025–26.
05
Institutional Governance
Establish Formal Pass Rate Targets and Embed This Dashboard in Governance Cycles
Set formal minimum course-level pass rate thresholds (e.g., 65% institutional floor, 70% aspirational target) and require Deans to present course-level performance data in end-of-term governance meetings. Courses below threshold for two or more consecutive semesters should trigger a formal academic review. Transparency and structured accountability are essential to converting insight into sustained improvement.
